Part 1 — Concepts (0–15 min)
\n

What even is AI?

\n

Before we touch any code, let\'s get one thing crystal clear: AI is not magic, and it\'s not a brain. It\'s a very sophisticated pattern-matching machine that was trained on enormous amounts of text.

\n
Real-world analogy

Imagine you read every book, article, and website ever written. Then someone asks you a question. You don\'t \'think\' — you remember patterns from everything you read and predict what a good answer looks like. That\'s essentially what AI does.

\n
🤖

AI (Artificial Intelligence)

The broad idea: machines doing tasks that normally require human thinking — like understanding language, recognising images, or making decisions.

📊

Machine Learning

A subset of AI. Instead of programming rules, you show the system millions of examples and it learns the patterns automatically.

💬

LLMs (Large Language Models)

The specific type of AI we use in this course — like Claude and Gemini. Trained on text, they predict what words come next. That\'s it. But done at massive scale, it\'s incredibly powerful.

🌡️

Generative AI

AI that creates new content — text, images, code — rather than just classifying or detecting things.

\n

Claude vs Gemini — what\'s the difference?

\n

Both are LLMs, both are excellent. Think of them like two brilliant friends who went to different schools:

\n
🟠

Claude (by Anthropic)

Exceptional at nuanced writing, careful reasoning, following complex instructions, and long documents. Built with safety as a core value.

🔵

Gemini (by Google)

Tightly integrated with Google\'s ecosystem — Search, Docs, Drive, Maps. Great multimodal abilities and strong coding support.

🛠️

When to use which

Use Claude for your AI logic and content generation. Use Google\'s APIs for infrastructure — search, storage, translation, voice. This course uses both.

\n

How does an LLM actually work? (No maths, promise)

\n
1

Training

Google and Anthropic fed their models trillions of words from the internet, books, and code. The model learned which words tend to follow which other words in which contexts.

2

Tokenisation

When you type text, it gets split into small chunks called tokens (roughly one word or part of a word each). The model processes tokens, not letters.

3

Prediction

Given your input tokens, the model predicts the most likely next token, then the next, then the next — building up a response one piece at a time.

4

Sampling

A \'temperature\' setting controls randomness. Low temperature = more predictable. High temperature = more creative. More on this in Hour 6.

\n
⚠️

Common misconception: AI doesn\'t \'look things up\' or \'think\'. It generates tokens based on training data. This is why it can confidently say wrong things — it predicts what sounds right, not what is factually true.

Part 2 — Hands-on Practice (15–45 min)
\n

Try it yourself

\n

No coding needed yet. Open two browser tabs and follow these exercises.

\n
1

Set up Claude

Go to claude.ai and create a free account. This is your primary AI tool for the course.

2

Set up Google AI Studio

Go to aistudio.google.com and sign in with your Google account. This gives you access to Gemini and later the API.

3

Your first experiment

In Claude, type exactly this: \'Explain photosynthesis to a 10-year-old in 3 sentences.\' Read the answer carefully.

4

Change the framing

Now type: \'Explain photosynthesis to a PhD botanist in 3 sentences.\' Notice how completely different the response is — same topic, different context.

5

Try Gemini

Open Google AI Studio, paste the same two prompts. Compare the style and tone. Neither is wrong — they\'re just different.

\n
\n
Exercise 1Spot the pattern
\n
\n

Ask Claude and Gemini the same question: \'What is gravity?\' Once with no context, and once with \'I am a 8-year-old student\'. Write down the differences you notice between: (1) the two models, and (2) the two prompts.

\n \n \n
Claude tends to be more nuanced and structured. Gemini often gives more concise answers. Both models significantly simplify when you provide the age context — they adjust vocabulary, examples, and sentence length. This is the core superpower of prompting: context shapes everything.
\n
\n
\n
\n
Exercise 2Test the limits
\n
\n

Ask Claude something that requires real-time knowledge, like \'What is the weather in Singapore right now?\' or \'Who won yesterday\'s football match?\'. What happens? Why?

\n \n \n
AI gives a confident-sounding but incorrect or outdated answer, or admits it doesn\'t have real-time data. This is a crucial limitation. AI was trained on data up to a certain date (the \'knowledge cutoff\'). It has no internet access unless explicitly given tools. This is why Hour 8 (Google Search + AI) is so important.

Part 3 — Review (45–60 min)
\n

Key takeaways from Hour 1

\n
✅

AI = pattern matching at scale

Not a brain. Not magic. Extremely sophisticated next-token prediction.

✅

Claude + Google = our stack

Claude for intelligence, Google APIs for infrastructure.

✅

Context changes everything

The same AI gives wildly different answers based on how you frame your question.

✅

AI has a knowledge cutoff

It doesn\'t know about recent events unless you tell it or connect it to search.

\n

Check your understanding

Answer all questions to see your score.

Q1. What is the most accurate description of how an LLM like Claude works?
✅ Correct! LLMs are token-prediction engines. They generate text that statistically fits the context — they don\'t retrieve facts or search the web (unless given special tools).
Q2. Why did Claude give different answers when you changed the audience from \'PhD\' to \'10-year-old\'?
✅ Correct! Context in a prompt activates different learned patterns. A \'PhD botanist\' context activates technical vocabulary and precise language; \'10-year-old\' activates simple vocabulary and relatable analogies.
Q3. Claude confidently tells you the current Prime Minister of a country, but it was wrong. What most likely happened?
✅ Correct! This is called a \'hallucination\' caused by a knowledge cutoff. The PM may have changed after Claude\'s training ended. Always verify AI facts about current events.
